## Why a Catamaran (and Not Just Any Boat)

Jeju has plenty of boat tours. Most are diesel-engine fishing-boat-style cruises that bounce hard and smell like fuel. A catamaran is a different animal — two hulls, mostly under sail, way more stable, and quiet enough to actually hear the wind.

Grande Bleu is Korea's only certified catamaran brand, which is a small but specific trust signal. It's not a knock-off speedboat tarted up with fairy lights.

The boat sails out of **Seogwipo Daepo Port** on Jeju's south coast — the side most travellers skip in favour of the busier north around Jeju City. That alone makes it feel less mass-tourist than equivalents in Bali's Benoa Harbour or Santorini's Ammoudi Bay.

## What You Actually Get for the Price

This is the part most blog posts skip. Here's what's included in the 1-hour cruise:

- Draft beer (yes, on tap, not warm cans)
- Wine
- Fresh Jeju tangerine juice
- Instant ramen cooked onboard
- Fishing gear — and you actually catch real fish: rockfish, scorpionfish, filefish
- Sunset views from open deck

## The Solo Healing Angle (Real Talk)

I went on this expecting it to be a couples thing. It's not. The boat seats up to **91 guests** across two decks, which means it's social enough to not feel awkward solo, but big enough that nobody's forcing icebreakers on you.

If you're doing the slow side of K-culture — the version that isn't Hongdae nightlife or Myeongdong shopping — this fits. It's the same energy as a quiet café in Aewol or a slow morning at a Jeju guesthouse. Sit on deck, beer in hand, no phone signal worth checking, watch Hallasan turn pink behind you.

That's the part I didn't expect: it's actually quiet. The sail cuts the engine noise, and people mostly chill out rather than party.

## Going in June, July, or August?

Summer is the sweet spot but comes with caveats. **Jeju June through August** is warm but also rainy season territory — late June through mid-July (jangma) can scrub trips. By August it clears up and you get long golden-hour sunsets around 7:30pm.

What to bring:

- Light jacket (wind picks up on the water even in August)
- Reef-safe sunscreen
- Motion sickness tablets if you're prone — catamarans are stable but the open sea is the open sea
- Polarised sunglasses for actually seeing the water
- A real camera or at least a clean phone lens

Book the sunset slot, not the daytime one. The light is the whole point.

## Instagrammable Without Being Cringe

The catamaran has the classic netted trampoline area between the two hulls — yes, the one you've seen on every yacht reel. The difference here is you're not fighting fifty influencers for the spot. It's a 91-person boat, not a 300-person party catamaran.

The deck angles work for photos basically the whole hour. Sunset behind, Hallasan to the north, open Pacific to the south. The volcanic coastline gives you a backdrop you genuinely can't fake with a preset.

Is it life-changing? No. But it's one of the rare Jeju activities that delivers exactly what it promises without inflating the experience. For a repeat visitor looking for something low-effort, photogenic, and genuinely relaxing, it's the easiest yes on the island.
